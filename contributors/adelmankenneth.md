# Contributor Report: Adelman, Kenneth

### Professional Overview
Kenneth Adelman served as the Deputy United States Ambassador to the United Nations (1981–1983) and as the Director of the Arms Control and Disarmament Agency (1983–1987) under the Reagan administration. He is frequently identified as a prominent member of the neoconservative movement and has held roles on the Pentagon's Defense Policy Board (2001–2006) during the George W. Bush administration. His career is characterized by an advocacy for "peace through strength" and a muscular U.S. foreign policy in the Middle East.

### Documentation of Zionism & Pro-Israel Advocacy

#### 1. Institutional Affiliations and Strategic Advocacy
Adelman has been a central figure in organizations that advocate for a close U.S.-Israel strategic alliance. He was a signatory of the April 3, 2002, open letter from the **Project for the New American Century (PNAC)** to President George W. Bush. The letter characterized Israel and the U.S. as "targets of what you have correctly called an 'Axis of Evil'" and demanded "steadfast support" for Israel. It explicitly urged the U.S. to cease pressuring Israel to negotiate with Yasser Arafat, whom the letter equated to Osama bin Laden.
*   **Source:** [Militarist Monitor: Letter to President Bush on Israel, Arafat and the War on Terrorism](https://militarist-monitor.org/profile/letter-to-president-bush-on-israel-arafat-and-the-war-on-terrorism-april-3-2002/)

He was also a member of the **Committee on the Present Danger (CPD)**, a group revived after 9/11 to frame the "war on terror" as a struggle against "radical Islam," a framework often used to align U.S. military interests with Israeli security objectives.
*   **Source:** [Wikispooks: Kenneth Adelman Affiliations](https://wikispooks.com/wiki/Kenneth_Adelman)

#### 2. Public Commentary and Media Advocacy
Adelman has used his media presence to defend Israeli military actions and criticize international detractors. In a July 2002 column titled "The Ankle Biters" for Fox News, Adelman lambasted European and Arab nations for their criticism of U.S. and Israeli policies. He argued that critics of the United States and its allies in the Middle East were engaging in "bitter carping" while offering little "militarily or intellectually."
*   **Source:** [Fox News: Readers React to NOW, Ankle Biters](https://www.foxnews.com/story/readers-react-to-now-ankle-biters)

In 1990, during a televised "Town Meeting" with Nelson Mandela on ABC's *Nightline*, Adelman publicly challenged Mandela for his support of Yasser Arafat and the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO). Adelman questioned how Mandela could view Arafat as a "model of human rights," a confrontation cited by scholars as an example of neoconservative efforts to delegitimize Palestinian leadership in U.S. discourse.
*   **Source:** [Boundary 2 Online: Ken Adelman and the Mandela Interview](https://www.boundary2.org/2014/01/ken-adelman-and-the-mandela-interview/)

#### 3. UN Tenure and Policy Defense
During his tenure as Deputy Ambassador to the UN, Adelman worked under Jeane Kirkpatrick, an ambassador noted for her staunchly pro-Israel stance. During this period, the U.S. delegation frequently used its veto power to block Security Council resolutions critical of Israeli military actions in Lebanon and the occupied territories. Adelman's role involved defending the "strategic asset" of Israel against what the administration termed the "politicization" of the UN by the Arab bloc.
*   **Source:** [Miller Center: Kenneth Adelman Oral History (UN and Reagan Era)](https://millercenter.org/the-presidency/presidential-oral-histories/kenneth-adelman-oral-history)
*   **Source:** [Cato Institute: No Tea Parties for "Bibi" (Neoconservative UN Background)](https://www.cato.org/commentary/no-tea-parties-bibi)

#### 4. Political Influence and Regional Conflict
Adelman was a leading proponent of the 2003 Iraq War, famously predicting it would be a "cakewalk." Critical analysts have noted that Adelman and other neoconservative figures viewed the removal of Saddam Hussein as a necessary step to reconfigure the Middle East to be "safe for Israel" and to eliminate a major regional adversary of the Zionist state.
